\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

All results in this section use the primary configuration of our
pipeline (corresponding to \texttt{adv.yaml} in the released
codebase): PaddleOCR as the detector, CoTracker3 online mode for
temporal propagation, AnyText2 as the text-editing backend, Hi-SAM
as the inpainter backend, and both the BPN and Alignment Refiner
active. Hardware is a single consumer-grade GPU. The evaluation
videos consist of real-world scenes captured with handheld or
low-rate camera motion, in natural lighting, and cover a mix of
Latin-to-Chinese and Latin-to-Spanish localization pairs.

\subsection{Qualitative comparison with a generative baseline}
\label{sec:exp:qualitative}

Figures~\ref{fig:cmp:warden} and~\ref{fig:cmp:highway} show
side-by-side qualitative comparisons between our pipeline and
Runway's Gen-4 Aleph~\citep{runway2025aleph}, an end-to-end
generative video editor. For each example we show the source
frame, Gen-4 Aleph's output when prompted for the equivalent
translation, and our pipeline's output. The two systems exhibit a
consistent pattern across examples. Gen-4 Aleph's output preserves
the photorealistic texture and lighting of the source surface---
wood grain, paint scuff, subtle lighting gradient---to a level
that is difficult to match with explicit compositing. At the same
time, its rendered glyphs are frequently unreadable or
semantically incorrect, for example producing unrelated Chinese
characters that are neither the source word nor its translation.
Our pipeline produces the correct target string in every tested
example, but inherits the coarser texture blending characteristic
of a compositing approach. We view these as complementary
capabilities rather than competing ones: Gen-4 Aleph excels at
naturalism, our pipeline excels at glyph correctness.

\subsection{Module ablations}
\label{sec:exp:ablation}

To assess the contribution of individual components we designed or
tuned, we disable them one at a time and measure three quantitative
metrics on a representative video set: (i)~\emph{OCR readback
accuracy}, defined as PaddleOCR's string-level match rate when
re-detecting the translated text in our output;
(ii)~\emph{background SSIM}~\citep{wang2004ssim} measured outside
the text alpha mask, which captures whether the pipeline damages
surrounding scene pixels; and (iii)~\emph{quad jitter}, the mean
per-frame corner displacement of the final composite, which
captures temporal stability. Table~\ref{tab:ablation} summarizes
the results.

We expect the strongest effects to fall on temporal stability
(Alignment Refiner) and readback accuracy (adaptive mask), since
these target the most direct failure modes of a naive pipeline.
Figures~\ref{fig:abl:bpn}, \ref{fig:abl:refiner}, and
\ref{fig:abl:lcm} give qualitative illustrations of each component's
contribution. The BPN's contribution is often visually clear even
when the numerical SSIM gap is modest, because motion blur matters
most at a small set of locally high-blur frames that averaging
tends to hide.

\subsection{Adaptive mask for long-to-short translation}
\label{sec:exp:adaptive}

Figure~\ref{fig:adaptive} isolates the adaptive-mask path of S3 on
a pair in which the target string is substantially shorter than the
source. Without the adaptive path, AnyText2 tends to fill the
oversized mask with plausible-looking but semantically incorrect
glyphs. With the adaptive path, the target renders at its natural
width, the remainder of the mask region contains a feathered
restoration of the original pixels, and the overall appearance is
clean.

\figplaceholder{adaptive_mask_before_after}{\linewidth}%
  {The adaptive-mask path in S3 for long-to-short translations.
   Left: the source canonical ROI with a long source string.
   Middle: the hybrid canvas after the configured background
   inpainter pre-clears the source text and a feathered centered
   strip of original pixels is restored at the target's natural
   width. Right: the AnyText2 output, rendered into the shrunk
   mask region, free of the gibberish-fill artifact common to
   mask-based editors when target length is much shorter than
   source.}%
  {fig:adaptive}

\subsection{Current limitations}
\label{sec:exp:limits}

Three limitations are worth noting explicitly. The most important
is the \emph{style-transfer gap} against end-to-end generative
baselines: by design, our pipeline produces correct target glyphs,
but the resulting texture match is not as naturalistic as Gen-4
Aleph's. We view this as the fundamental tradeoff of the
glyph-controlled paradigm. Second, \emph{temporal stability} is
imperfect even with the Alignment Refiner: homographies are
estimated per-frame and the refiner predicts per-frame
independently, which leaves small micro-oscillations in the
composited corners that are sometimes visible on long clips.
Third, the adaptive mask's character-class width heuristic is an
approximation: highly stylized fonts whose glyph widths deviate
substantially from the heuristic averages can still leave a small
residual artifact at the mask edges. All three are promising
directions for future work and do not affect the feasibility
result.
